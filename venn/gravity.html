    I. Early Life and Education

    Newton was born in 1643 in Woolsthorpe, England. He was a precocious child who showed interest in mathematics, mechanics and asstronomy. He studied at Trinity College, Cambridge, where he became a fellow in 1667 and a professor in 1669.



    Image 3

    II. Newton's Religious Beliefs

    Newton was a devout but heterodox Christian who rejected the doctrine of the Trinity and believed in a monotheistic God as the masterful creator of the universe. He studied the Bible extensively and wrote many religious tracts that dealt with prophecy, chronology and the origins of Christianity. He was influenced by Socinian and Arian views and kept his heretical beliefs private. [2][3]

    Image 4

    III. Newtons's Scientific Discoveries

    Newton is widely regarded as one of the greatest scientists of all time. He formulated the laws of motion and universal gravitation, invented calculus, built the first reflecting telescope, and discovered the spectrum of light and the theory of color. He also made contributions to optics, fluid mechanics, alchemy and theology.


    Image 5
    IV. Later Life and Legacy

    Newton was knighted in 1705 and served as the president of the Royal Society and the master of the Mint. He died in 1727 and was buried in Westminster Abbey. He is widely recognized as one of the most influential figures in the history of science, mathematics and philosophy. [1]
